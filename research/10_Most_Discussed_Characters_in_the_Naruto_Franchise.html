Abstract

The Naruto franchise spanning Naruto, Naruto Shippuden, and Boruto: Naruto Next Generations, commands one of the most active anime communities on YouTube. This study applies sentiment analysis to 3,309 YouTube comments collected from 46 franchise-related videos using the YouTube Data API v3. Comments were preprocessed, and character mentions were identified via name-matching and alias resolution. The ten most frequently mentioned characters were selected, and VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) was applied to classify comment sentiment as positive, negative, or neutral. Results indicate that Naruto Uzumaki leads in discussion volume (590 mentions, avg. sentiment +0.121), while Pain is the sole character with an overall negative sentiment score (avg. −0.176). Legacy characters from the original series and Shippuden dominate fan discourse, suggesting that nostalgia and narrative depth remain primary drivers of audience engagement. This study demonstrates the viability of YouTube comment mining as a method for quantifying anime character popularity and fan perception.

I. Introduction

Anime franchises occupy a unique position in global popular culture, generating sustained fan engagement that extends well beyond the original broadcast period. The Naruto franchise, created by Masashi Kishimoto and expanded through Naruto Shippuden and Boruto: Naruto Next Generations, is among the most globally recognized anime properties, with a substantial and vocal online community [1].

YouTube has emerged as a primary platform for fan discourse, hosting character discussions, reaction videos, retrospectives, and ranking debates. The comment sections of these videos constitute a rich, largely unstructured corpus of audience opinion. Sentiment analysis, as a branch of natural language processing (NLP), provides a systematic mechanism for quantifying the emotional polarity embedded within such text data [2].

Prior research has applied sentiment analysis to social media platforms including Twitter and Reddit for anime discourse [3], yet YouTube despite its dominance as a video platform remains underutilized as a research corpus in this domain. This study addresses that gap by focusing on YouTube comment data to measure character salience and audience sentiment within the Naruto universe.

II. Objectives

The specific objectives of this study are:

O1. To identify the ten most frequently discussed Naruto franchise characters in YouTube comment data.

O2. To classify the sentiment of comments mentioning each character as positive, negative, or neutral using VADER.

O3. To compare character popularity and fan sentiment, and to interpret patterns in relation to character narrative roles across the three series.

III. Methodology

A. Data Collection

Data were collected via the YouTube Data API v3. Search queries employed franchise-related keywords including character names, arc titles, and series identifiers. A total of 46 videos were selected based on relevance, and 3,309 user comments were retrieved. Each comment was associated with its parent video, upload date, and like count.

B. Preprocessing

Raw comment text was cleaned by removing URLs, special characters, HTML entities, and excess whitespace. Text was lowercased to ensure consistent matching. Comments were retained only if they contained at least one recognizable character reference.

C. Character Identification

Character mentions were identified through a curated dictionary of canonical names and commonly used aliases (e.g., "Minato" for the Fourth Hokage, "Tobi" for Obito). Mention counts were aggregated per character to determine the top ten most discussed individuals.

D. Sentiment Analysis (VADER Lexicon Scoring)

VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) was applied to each comment. VADER produces a compound score ranging from −1.0 (maximally negative) to +1.0 (maximally positive). Following convention [4], a compound score ≥ 0.05 was classified as positive, ≤ −0.05 as negative, and the remainder as neutral. The average compound score and distribution of classifications were computed for each of the ten characters.

V. Discussion

Naruto Uzumaki's position as both the most mentioned and highest-scoring character (avg. +0.121) reflects the franchise's foundational audience attachment to the protagonist. His narrative arc, from social outcast to Hokage, has generated sustained emotional investment among fans, a finding consistent with research linking protagonist identification with positive online engagement [5].

Itachi Uchiha achieved the second-highest average sentiment score (+0.115) despite ranking fourth in total mentions, suggesting that while his discussion volume is somewhat lower, the quality of emotional engagement is high. This aligns with the character's cultural status as a fan-favorite "tragic hero" whose backstory resonated deeply with the audience.

Key Finding 1: Legacy characters from Naruto and Shippuden (Naruto, Sasuke, Itachi, Kakashi) dominate both mention frequency and positive sentiment, suggesting that nostalgia and long-form narrative investment are primary drivers of fan engagement.

The near-tie between Sakura and Obito in mention count (both 79) but divergent sentiment scores (+0.060 vs. −0.004) is noteworthy. Obito's classification as Neutral/Mixed may reflect audience ambivalence toward his role as both a villain and a sympathetic character, whereas Sakura's discussions, though proportionally including notable negative commentary (31.6%), trend slightly positive overall.

Key Finding 2: Pain is the only character with an overall negative sentiment (avg. −0.176), with 60.7% of comments classified as negative. This may reflect the nature of fan discussions about villains particularly those whose actions, while narratively impactful, are evaluated unfavorably in isolation from story context by VADER.

Kakashi Hatake's unusually high neutral proportion (47.3%) may indicate that discussions about this character tend toward analytical or informational commentary rather than strong emotional expression consistent with the character's calm, mentor-like persona.

The two Boruto-era characters in the top ten, Boruto Uzumaki (rank 3) and Kawaki (rank 7) both received positive sentiment classifications. However, their mention counts are substantially lower than the legacy characters, indicating that franchise longevity and emotional legacy from older series continue to shape YouTube discourse patterns.

VI. Conclusion

This study presented a YouTube comment-based sentiment analysis of the ten most discussed characters in the Naruto franchise. By collecting 3,309 comments from 46 videos via the YouTube Data API v3 and applying the VADER sentiment analysis tool, the study achieved its stated objectives:

The ten most discussed characters were identified, with Naruto Uzumaki at the top (590 mentions). Sentiment was classified for each character, revealing that eight of ten characters received an overall positive classification. Comparative analysis demonstrated that legacy characters from the original series and Shippuden dominate both discussion volume and sentiment scores.

The findings suggest that YouTube comment mining is a viable and informative methodology for studying audience perception of fictional characters in long-running media franchises. The persistence of legacy characters in fan discourse underscores the role of nostalgia, character depth, and narrative arc quality in sustaining audience engagement.

VII. Limitations

Several constraints limit the generalizability of the present findings. The analysis is confined to YouTube and does not incorporate data from Reddit, Twitter/X, or other platforms. VADER, while robust for general-purpose sentiment detection, may misclassify comments that employ sarcasm, anime-specific slang, or multi-lingual expressions common in international fan communities. Character name-matching may undercount references via indirect means (plot role descriptions, character archetypes). The sample size of 46 videos, while sufficient for a pilot study, may not be representative of the full YouTube discourse around the franchise.

VIII. Future Work

Future research should expand data collection to multiple social media platforms to enable cross-platform comparison. The deployment of transformer-based models such as BERT or RoBERTa would likely improve sentiment classification accuracy on domain-specific text. Temporal analysis, tracking sentiment evolution across broadcast periods or major story arcs would add a longitudinal dimension. Additionally, separating the analysis by series (Naruto, Shippuden, Boruto) would allow for arc-specific comparisons of character reception.
